12th century

Origins & Construction

Angkor Wat was built in the early 12th century (1113–1150 AD) during the reign of King Suryavarman II. Originally dedicated to the Hindu god Vishnu, it served as both the king's state temple and mausoleum. Built at the height of the Khmer Empire, it showcases the empire's architectural, engineering, and religious achievements. Today, it remains one of the world's greatest ancient monuments and a symbol of Cambodia's rich cultural heritage.

Powerful Civilization

The Khmer Empire

The Khmer Empire was a powerful Southeast Asian civilization that flourished from the 9th to the 15th century. Known for its achievements in architecture, engineering, art, and religion, it shaped Cambodia's cultural identity. Its capital, Angkor, was the empire's political, religious, and cultural center. At its peak, the empire stretched across present-day Cambodia, Thailand, Laos, and Vietnam, with Angkor Wat as its greatest architectural achievement.

The legendary of Ancient People

King Suryavarman II

Meet the visionary ruler who built Angkor Wat in the early 12th century. King Suryavarman II united the Khmer Empire, expanded its influence, and created one of the world's greatest architectural masterpieces, leaving a legacy that continues to inspire generations.

Vishnu, The Hindu God

Discover the Hindu god Vishnu, the divine protector and preserver of the universe. Angkor Wat was originally dedicated to Vishnu, whose stories and symbols are beautifully carved throughout the temple, reflecting faith, power, and the rich spiritual beliefs of the Khmer Empire.

Khmer Angkor People

Discover the people who built and sustained the Khmer Empire. Skilled artisans, engineers, soldiers, and farmers worked together to create Angkor's magnificent temples, advanced irrigation systems, and thriving cities, leaving a cultural legacy that continues to shape Cambodia today.

Temple City

Architectural Achievement

Angkor Wat is the world's largest religious monument, covering about 400 acres (162 hectares). Its name means "Temple City." Unlike most Khmer temples, it faces west, possibly symbolizing death and the afterlife as King Suryavarman II's mausoleum. Its design represents Mount Meru, the sacred mountain in Hindu mythology, with its central towers symbolizing the mountain and the surrounding galleries representing the universe.

Cultural Symbol

Religious Transformation

Angkor Wat was originally built as a Hindu temple dedicated to Vishnu under King Suryavarman II. In the 13th century, it gradually became a Buddhist temple as Theravada Buddhism spread across the Khmer Empire. Buddhist statues were added alongside the original Hindu carvings, reflecting both religious traditions. Today, Angkor Wat remains an active Buddhist site and a symbol of Cambodia's history, faith, and culture.

Empire's Fall

Decline & Abandonment

Angkor Wat declined with the fall of the Khmer Empire in the 15th century, when the capital moved to present-day Phnom Penh. Although it remained a religious site, much of the surrounding city was abandoned and gradually covered by dense jungle. Despite years of neglect, Angkor Wat survived as a lasting symbol of Cambodia's history and cultural heritage.

Symbolism and Faith

Mount Meru

Explore the legendary Mount Meru, the sacred mountain at the center of Hindu mythology. Angkor Wat was designed to symbolize Mount Meru, representing the home of the gods. Its towering central spires reflect this sacred peak, highlighting the temple's deep spiritual and symbolic significance.

Theravada Buddhism

Learn about Theravada Buddhism, the dominant religion in Cambodia today. During the late Angkor period, Angkor Wat gradually changed from a Hindu temple into a Buddhist place of worship. It remains an active religious site, reflecting centuries of spiritual devotion and cultural heritage.

King Suryavarman II's Mausoleum

Discover the final resting place believed to honor King Suryavarman II, the visionary ruler who commissioned Angkor Wat. Many historians believe the temple also served as his mausoleum, combining royal remembrance with religious devotion and symbolizing his journey to the divine after death.
