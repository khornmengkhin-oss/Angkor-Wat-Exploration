The History Of Angkor Wat Temple

Trace Angkor Wat from its 12th-century origins to its place as Cambodia's most recognized cultural symbol.

Home / History

Origins & Construction

Angkor Wat Temple was built in the early 12th century, around 1113 to 1150 AD, during the reign of King Suryavarman II. It was originally constructed as a Hindu temple dedicated to the god Vishnu and served as the king's state temple as well as his eventual mausoleum. The temple was built by the Khmer Empire at the height of its power, reflecting the empire's remarkable achievements in architecture, engineering, and religious devotion. Today, Angkor Wat stands as one of the greatest monuments of the ancient world and remains a symbol of Cambodia's rich cultural heritage.

The Khmer Empire

The Khmer Empire was one of the most powerful and influential civilizations in Southeast Asia, flourishing from the 9th to the 15th century. The Khmer people were known for their remarkable achievements in architecture, engineering, art, and religion, which continue to shape Cambodia's cultural identity today. Angkor, located in present-day Siem Reap, served as the capital of the empire and was the political, religious, and cultural center of Khmer civilization. At its peak, the Khmer Empire extended its influence across much of Southeast Asia, including areas of present-day Cambodia, Thailand, Laos, and Vietnam. This period of prosperity and power provided the foundation for the construction of magnificent monuments, with Angkor Wat standing as the empire's most iconic achievement.

Architectural Achievement

Angkor Wat is widely recognized as the largest religious monument in the world, covering an area of approximately 400 acres (about 162 hectares). The name "Angkor Wat" is derived from the Khmer language and is commonly translated as "Temple City" or "City of Temples." One of the temple's most distinctive features is its west-facing orientation, which is unusual among Khmer temples that typically face east. Historians believe this unique direction may be associated with death and the afterlife, reflecting its role as the mausoleum of King Suryavarman II. The temple's architectural design is also deeply symbolic, representing Mount Meru, the sacred mountain believed in Hindu mythology to be the home of the gods. Its towering central spires and surrounding galleries were carefully designed to symbolize this cosmic mountain and the universe surrounding it.

Religious Transformation

Angkor Wat was originally built as a Hindu temple dedicated to Vishnu and was an important center of Vaishnavism during the reign of King Suryavarman II. However, beginning in the 13th century, as Theravada Buddhism gradually became the dominant religion in the Khmer Empire, the temple was transformed into a Buddhist place of worship. This religious transition can be seen throughout the temple, where Buddhist statues and images were added alongside the original Hindu carvings and architectural features. Rather than replacing its earlier heritage, Angkor Wat came to reflect both Hindu and Buddhist traditions, showcasing the rich spiritual history of Cambodia. Today, the temple remains an active Buddhist site where monks, worshippers, and visitors from around the world continue to gather, making it not only a historical monument but also a living center of faith and culture.

Decline & Abandonment

The decline of Angkor Wat was closely connected to the fall of the Khmer Empire during the 15th century. As political and economic conditions changed, the empire gradually lost its power, and the royal capital was moved south to the area that is now Phnom Penh. Although Angkor Wat continued to serve as an important religious site, much of the surrounding city and temple complex was gradually abandoned. Over the centuries, dense jungle vegetation slowly reclaimed many of the structures, covering them with towering trees and thick roots. Despite this period of neglect, Angkor Wat endured the passage of time and remained one of the most remarkable symbols of Cambodia's history and cultural heritage.

Rediscovery by the West

Although Angkor Wat was never truly lost to the Cambodian people, it became widely known to the Western world after the French explorer Henri Mouhot visited the site and published his accounts in 1860. His descriptions of the temple's magnificent architecture and mysterious atmosphere captured the imagination of readers across Europe, bringing international attention to this remarkable monument. During the French colonial period, extensive restoration and conservation efforts were undertaken to preserve the temple and protect it from further deterioration. Throughout the late 19th and 20th centuries, Angkor Wat continued to gain worldwide recognition, attracting scholars, historians, and travelers from around the globe. Today, it is celebrated as one of the greatest architectural and cultural treasures in human history.

Modern Era

The modern history of Angkor Wat has been marked by both challenges and remarkable preservation efforts. During the 1970s, Cambodia experienced years of conflict, including the Khmer Rouge period and the civil war, which caused damage to many historical sites and disrupted conservation work at the temple complex. Despite these difficulties, Angkor Wat survived and continued to symbolize the resilience of the Cambodian people. In 1992, UNESCO designated Angkor Wat and the surrounding Angkor Archaeological Park as a World Heritage Site, recognizing their outstanding cultural and historical value. Since then, restoration and preservation projects have been carried out through international cooperation, with countries such as France, Japan, India, and many others contributing their expertise to protect this priceless heritage. Today, Angkor Wat is Cambodia's most popular tourist destination and serves as a powerful national symbol, proudly appearing on the Cambodian flag as a representation of the nation's history, identity, and enduring spirit.
