Angkor Wat

Architectural Overview

Angkor Wat combines the temple-mountain design with concentric galleries. Its layout and orientation may have celestial meaning, including alignments with the sunrise at solstices. Many interpret the temple as a symbol of Mount Meru, with the central towers representing the mountain's peaks and the surrounding walls and moat representing mountains and ocean. It is unusual because it faces west, not east. Some scholars think this means it was intended as a funerary temple for Suryavarman, supported by the counter-clockwise bas-reliefs associated with funeral rites. Others argue the westward orientation reflects its dedication to Vishnu, who is linked with the west. Overall, the temple's layout, dimensions, and alignments have led to many deeper symbolic interpretations.

Main Architectural Elements

What makes Angkor Wat unique?

The temple's main elements include the causeway, galleries, courtyards and platforms, and towers and stairways

Causeway

192 meters long and 11.6 meters wide, made out of sandstone blocks, it connects the temple approach to the main enclosure and helps guide movement along the central axis. It leads visitors from the western entrance across the water to the main temple complex.

Galleries

Combine the tiered pyramidal structure of a temple-mountain with South Indian-inspired concentric corridors. Three raised, rectangular galleries wrap around the central quincunx of towers, acting as structural borders for the stepped terraces and serving as a physical manifestation of Mount Meru.

Courtyards and Platforms

nested, concentric square enclosures that progressively elevate toward a central quincunx of towers. Featuring cruciform layouts, colonnaded galleries, and traditional libraries, these spaces were intricately designed to mirror Hindu cosmology.

Towers and Stairways

The five central, lotus-bud towers rise to 65 meters, arranged in a quincunx. The famously steep staircases, inclined up to 70 degrees, were designed to be spiritually challenging, forcing a physical posture of submission.
